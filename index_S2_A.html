At the Royal Albert Hall, Smith morphed between rock goddess, incantatory shaman and comedian

By Fiona Sturgest
October 6, 2021 1:43 pm (Updated 4:09 pm)
Email Twitter WhatsApp Facebook

“I always imagined when I came to the Royal Albert Hall that I was going to do everything perfect, no f***in’ around,” says Patti Smith, icon of American punk, after mangling the intro to “My Blakean Year”.

But even an imperfect Smith is a brilliant Smith, as illustrated by her performance of Bob Dylan’s “A Hard Rain’s Gonna Fall” at the 2016 Nobel prize ceremony in Sweden, when she forgot the lyrics and had to start again. Her second attempt reduced the room to tears and subsequently went viral.

Smith remains a reluctant rock star – she calls herself a writer rather than musician, in tribute to those she believes do the latter job better than her. Yet, at 74, she is one of the most visceral and charismatic performers out there.

Her two nights at the Albert Hall, with a band that includes her son, Jackson Smith, and long-serving guitarist Lenny Kaye, were originally scheduled for spring 2020, before the pandemic brought live music to a halt.

This evening she hails the patience and optimism of those who held on to their tickets, earnestly dedicating the 2000 song “Grateful” to them.
Smith’s setlist hopscotches across the years, drawing on her vast and varied catalogue and occasionally dipping into other people’s – there are covers of Stevie Wonder’s “Blame It On The Sun” and Dylan’s “One Too Many Mornings”.

Over two hours, Smith seamlessly morphs between rock goddess, incantatory shaman and … comedian? When someone yelps “I love you, Patti!” she drawls, “I’m not available.” Another fan raises the subject of her now-fabled love of British detective dramas such as Inspector George Gently, Morse and Midsomer Murders.

Smith confirms she loves them all before launching into an improvised ode to her favourite television network – “When I’m home in NYC, I think only of you ITV3”.

Horses, Smith’s landmark album from 1975 which fused punk and poetry, looms large, with “Land: Horses/Land of a Thousand Dances” merging exquisitely with “Gloria (In Excelsis Deo)”, with the unforgettable line “Jesus died for somebody’s sins but not mine.” These songs sound as hypnotic and transgressive as they did 45 years ago.

Elsewhere, “Free Money” reminds you that, for all her poetic rapture, Smith can crank out a rock banger when the mood takes her. Similarly raucous and joyful is 1978’s “Because The Night”, for which the lights are turned up to reveal a crowd on their feet and punching the air.

Smith’s sense of wonder at being back on stage and communing with the faithful rarely leaves her face, her energy undimmed after nearly two years confined to her home.

A reluctant rock star she may be, but it’s good to have her back.
